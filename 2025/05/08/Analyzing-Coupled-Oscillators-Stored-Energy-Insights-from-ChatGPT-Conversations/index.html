    Analyzing Coupled Oscillators' Stored Energy: Insights from ChatGPT Conversations

    Posted on 2025-05-08 In note Views: Disqus:
    How ChatGPT Explains Energy Transfer in Coupled Oscillators

    User:

    We have two coupled oscillators with loss, one oscillator has large stored energy and very small loss, and the other has very small stored energy and large loss. Please give the formular of the eigen mode frequencies of this coupled oscillation system, and show the stored energy relation of the two oscillators by using the coupling coefficient. Please also give the definition of the coupling coefficient.

    chatGPT:

    To analyze the system of two coupled oscillators with loss, we need to:

    1. Derive the eigenmode frequencies of the system.
    2. Relate the stored energies in the two oscillators using the coupling coefficient.
    3. Define the coupling coefficient.

    System Description

    Let:

    • Oscillator 1 have large stored energy, small loss.
    • Oscillator 2 have small stored energy, large loss.

    Let the complex amplitudes of the oscillators be $a_1(t)$ and $a_2(t)$, and the system can be modeled by the coupled-mode equations:

    $$
    \begin{cases}
    \frac{d a_1}{dt} = -(i \omega_1 + \gamma_1) a_1 - i \kappa a_2 \
    \frac{d a_2}{dt} = -(i \omega_2 + \gamma_2) a_2 - i \kappa a_1
    \end{cases}
    $$

    Where:

    • $\omega_1$, $\omega_2$: resonance frequencies of the uncoupled oscillators,
    • $\gamma_1$, $\gamma_2$: damping rates (with $\gamma_1 \ll \gamma_2$),
    • $\kappa$: real coupling rate.

    1. Eigenmode Frequencies

    Assume time-harmonic solutions $a_i(t) = A_i e^{-i\Omega t}$, substitute into the equations:

    $$
    \begin{pmatrix}
    \Omega - \omega_1 + i\gamma_1 & \kappa \
    \kappa & \Omega - \omega_2 + i\gamma_2
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    A_1 \ A_2
    \end{pmatrix}
    = 0
    $$

    For nontrivial solutions, the determinant must vanish:

    $$
    (\Omega - \omega_1 + i\gamma_1)(\Omega - \omega_2 + i\gamma_2) - \kappa^2 = 0
    $$

    This quadratic equation in $\Omega$ gives the eigenfrequencies:

    $$
    \Omega_{\pm} = \frac{\omega_1 + \omega_2}{2} - i\frac{\gamma_1 + \gamma_2}{2} \pm \sqrt{ \kappa^2 - \left( \frac{\Delta\omega - i \Delta\gamma}{2} \right)^2 }
    $$

    Where:

    • $\Delta\omega = \omega_1 - \omega_2$,
    • $\Delta\gamma = \gamma_1 - \gamma_2$.

    2. Stored Energy Ratio and Coupling Coefficient

    Let the eigenmode amplitudes be $A_1$, $A_2$. For eigenmode $\Omega$, from the first equation:

    $$
    (\Omega - \omega_1 + i\gamma_1) A_1 + \kappa A_2 = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{A_2}{A_1} = - \frac{ \Omega - \omega_1 + i\gamma_1 }{ \kappa }
    $$

    The stored energy is proportional to $|A_i|^2$, so:

    $$
    \frac{U_2}{U_1} = \left| \frac{A_2}{A_1} \right|^2 = \left| \frac{ \Omega - \omega_1 + i\gamma_1 }{ \kappa } \right|^2
    $$

    This relates the energy in the lossy oscillator to the low-loss oscillator using the coupling coefficient $\kappa$.

    3. Coupling Coefficient Definition

    The coupling coefficient $\kappa$ quantifies the strength of energy exchange between the oscillators and can be defined as:

    $$
    \kappa = \frac{\omega}{2} \cdot k
    $$

    Where:

    • $\omega$ is the uncoupled resonance frequency (if $\omega_1 \approx \omega_2$),
    • $k$ is the normalized coupling coefficient, often defined in resonator theory as:

    $$
    k = \frac{\omega_2 - \omega_1}{\omega_0}, \quad \text{(if coupled mode frequencies are known)}
    $$

    Alternatively, if the system is driven externally, $k$ can be defined via energy exchange rate per cycle:

    $$
    k = \frac{P_{\text{exchanged}}}{\omega U}
    $$

    Summary

    • Eigenfrequencies:

      $$
      \Omega_{\pm} = \frac{\omega_1 + \omega_2}{2} - i\frac{\gamma_1 + \gamma_2}{2} \pm \sqrt{ \kappa^2 - \left( \frac{\Delta\omega - i \Delta\gamma}{2} \right)^2 }
      $$

    • Stored energy ratio:

      $$
      \frac{U_2}{U_1} = \left| \frac{ \Omega - \omega_1 + i\gamma_1 }{ \kappa } \right|^2
      $$

    • Coupling coefficient $\kappa$: rate of energy exchange between oscillators.

    Would you like a numerical example to illustrate this system’s behavior?

    已思考 27 秒鐘

    Below is a concrete numerical example illustrating an eigenmode whose energy is 50× stored in oscillator 1 versus oscillator 2, i.e.

    $$
    \frac{U_{1}}{U_{2}} = 50.
    $$

    1. Parameter choice

    • Resonance of Osc 1:
      $\displaystyle \omega_{1} = 2\pi\times1.00;\text{GHz} \approx 6.2830\times10^{9},\mathrm{rad/s}$

    • Resonance of Osc 2 detuned slightly above:

      $$
      \Delta\omega ;=;\omega_{2}-\omega_{1}
      ;=;6.9512\times10^{6},\mathrm{rad/s}
      \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
      \omega_{2}=6.2830\times10^{9}+6.9512\times10^{6}.
      $$

    • Negligible losses: $\gamma_{1}=\gamma_{2}=0$.

    • Coupling rate:
      $\displaystyle \kappa = 1.00\times10^{6},\mathrm{rad/s}.$

    2. Eigen‐frequencies

    With $\Delta\omega = \omega_{2}-\omega_{1}$ and $\gamma_i=0$, the two eigenfrequencies are

    $$
    \Omega_{\pm}
    =\frac{\omega_{1}+\omega_{2}}{2};\pm;\sqrt{\kappa^{2} + \Bigl(\tfrac{\Delta\omega}{2}\Bigr)^{2}}
    ;=;\omega_{\rm avg};\pm;R,
    $$

    where

    $$
    \omega_{\rm avg} = \frac{6.2830\times10^{9}+6.2899512\times10^{9}}{2}
    =6.2864756\times10^{9},\mathrm{rad/s},
    \qquad
    R = \sqrt{(1.00\times10^{6})^{2} + (3.4756\times10^{6})^{2}}
    \approx 3.6170\times10^{6},\mathrm{rad/s}.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \Omega_{+} &\approx 6.2864756\times10^{9} + 3.6170\times10^{6}
    = 6.2900926\times10^{9},\mathrm{rad/s},\
    \Omega_{-} &\approx 6.2864756\times10^{9} - 3.6170\times10^{6}
    = 6.2828586\times10^{9},\mathrm{rad/s}.
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    In hertz, dividing by $2\pi$:

    $$
    f_{+}\approx1.0011;\text{GHz},\quad
    f_{-}\approx0.9995;\text{GHz}.
    $$

    3. Energy‐ratio calculation

    For each eigenmode $\Omega$, the modal amplitude ratio is

    $$
    \frac{A_{2}}{A_{1}}
    = -\frac{\Omega - \omega_{1}}{\kappa},
    $$

    so the stored‐energy ratio is

    $$
    \frac{U_{2}}{U_{1}}
    = \Bigl\lvert \tfrac{A_{2}}{A_{1}}\Bigr\rvert^{2}
    = \Bigl(\tfrac{\lvert \Omega - \omega_{1}\rvert}{\kappa}\Bigr)^{2}.
    $$

    Choose the lower eigenmode $\Omega_-,$ (closest to $\omega_1$). Then

    $$
    \bigl\lvert \Omega_- - \omega_{1}\bigr\rvert
    = \Bigl\lvert,\omega_{\rm avg} - R - \omega_{1}\Bigr\rvert
    = \bigl\lvert \tfrac{\Delta\omega}{2} - R \bigr\rvert
    = \bigl\lvert 3.4756\times10^{6} - 3.6170\times10^{6}\bigr\rvert
    = 1.414\times10^{5},\mathrm{rad/s}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \frac{U_{2}}{U_{1}}
    = \Bigl(\tfrac{1.414\times10^{5}}{1.00\times10^{6}}\Bigr)^{2}
    \approx0.02,
    \quad
    \Longrightarrow
    \quad
    \frac{U_{1}}{U_{2}} \approx 50.
    $$

    Summary table

    Parameter Value
    $\omega_{1}/2\pi$ 1.000 GHz
    $\omega_{2}/2\pi$ $1.000 + 0.001106$,GHz
    Coupling $\kappa$ $1.0\times10^{6}$,rad/s
    Eigen freqs $\Omega_{-}/2\pi$ 0.9995 GHz
    Energy ratio $U_{1}/U_{2}$ 50

    This shows explicitly how a modest detuning $\Delta\omega\simeq7\times10^6$ rad/s and weak coupling $\kappa=10^6$ rad/s produce a mode concentrated 50× more in the high-Q oscillator 1 than in the low-Q oscillator 2.

    chatGPT:

    已思考 2 秒鐘

    The key point is that both expressions

    $$
    \frac{U_2}{U_1}
    =\Bigl|\frac{A_2}{A_1}\Bigr|^2
    =\Bigl(\frac{|\Omega-\omega_1|}{\kappa}\Bigr)^2
    \quad\text{and}\quad
    \frac{U_1}{U_2}
    =\Bigl|\frac{A_1}{A_2}\Bigr|^2
    =\Bigl(\frac{|\Omega-\omega_2|}{\kappa}\Bigr)^2
    $$

    are equivalent once you invoke the eigenvalue (characteristic) equation

    $$
    (\Omega-\omega_1)(\Omega-\omega_2) ;=;\kappa^2.
    $$

    Why they give reciprocals

    From the determinant condition for non-trivial $A_1,A_2$,

    $$
    (\Omega-\omega_1)(\Omega-\omega_2)-\kappa^2=0
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    (\Omega-\omega_1)(\Omega-\omega_2)=\kappa^2.
    $$

    Taking absolute values,

    $$
    |\Omega-\omega_2|
    =\frac{\kappa^2}{|\Omega-\omega_1|},
    $$

    so

    $$
    \Bigl(\frac{|\Omega-\omega_2|}{\kappa}\Bigr)^2
    =\Bigl(\frac{\kappa}{|\Omega-\omega_1|}\Bigr)^2
    =\frac{1}{\bigl(\tfrac{|\Omega-\omega_1|}{\kappa}\bigr)^2}.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    \Bigl(\tfrac{|\Omega-\omega_2|}{\kappa}\Bigr)^2
    =\frac{1}{\bigl(\tfrac{|\Omega-\omega_1|}{\kappa}\bigr)^2}
    ;\Longrightarrow;
    \frac{U_1}{U_2}
    =\frac{1}{,U_2/U_1,}.
    $$

    Which formula to use?

    • If you already know $\displaystyle\frac{U_2}{U_1}=(|\Omega-\omega_1|/\kappa)^2$, then take its reciprocal to get $U_1/U_2$.
    • Equivalently, you can start from $\displaystyle U_1/U_2=(|\Omega-\omega_2|/\kappa)^2$.

    They’re just two ways of expressing the same thing, linked by the characteristic equation
    $;(\Omega-\omega_1)(\Omega-\omega_2)=\kappa^2.$

    Here’s a fully damped‐oscillator version of our previous example. We choose the same detuning and coupling, but now add small loss in oscillator 1 and large loss in oscillator 2:

    $$
    \begin{aligned}

    \omega_{2} &= \omega_{1} + 6.9512\times10^{6},\mathrm{rad/s},\
    \kappa &= 1.00\times10^{6},\mathrm{rad/s},\

    \end{aligned}

    \sqrt{,
    \kappa^{2}
    ;-;\Bigl(\frac{\Delta\omega - i,\Delta\gamma}{2}\Bigr)^{2}

    $$

    For each mode $\Omega$:

    $$
    \frac{A_2}{A_1}

    \frac{U_2}{U_1}
    =\Bigl\lvert\tfrac{A_2}{A_1}\Bigr\rvert^2

    and
    $\tfrac{U_1}{U_2}=(U_2/U_1)^{-1}.$

    3. Interpretation

    • Lower mode $\Omega_-$:
      Despite the detuning favoring localization in oscillator 1, the heavy loss in oscillator 2 makes the energy even more skewed—about 74× more energy stored in oscillator 1 than in oscillator 2.

      Very similar skew—about 75× more in oscillator 1—since both detuning and the large loss in oscillator 2 work in the same direction.

    This illustrates how adding damping shifts the modal energies further toward the high-Q (low-loss) oscillator.
